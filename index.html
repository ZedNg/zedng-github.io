Bubbles (2020)

Sonic Arts
Bubbles (2020)

"Life is an illusion, a dream, a bubble, a shadow.

Nothing is real.

Nothing is worthy of anger.

Nothing is worthy of disputes.

Nothing."

Buddha

"Bubbles" is a set of sonic arts experiments to produce an ambient soundtrack and a visual experience using sounds made from field recordings. The sounds I used were captured from my daily life and modulated used Adobe Audition to master the piece.

The soundtrack aims to produce a feeling of wonder with a sense of uncertainty and anxiety. Bubbles float freely in the air and creating different visual effects with lights from the surrounding. However, bubbles also have a fragile existence and pop without warning.

It is meant to serve as an analogy about life. We move and live freely, and sometimes aimlessly, during our lifetime where we enjoy our freedom and interact with our environment. But also like a bubble, life does come abruptly to an end. It is something I find people should keep in mind.

Another reason for the name is that there is no angry way to say the word “Bubbles”.

From the soundtrack, I produced “Bubbles No. 0”. It is a prelude of the track “Bubbles”. It consists of audio elements used in "Bubbles" and rearranged to provide a short rendition of the original piece.

Visually, this piece is an exploration of using bright and highly contrasting colours and simple shapes to create fascinating visuals. Also, the effects of the visual elements were generated and controlled by the computer’s interpretation of the audio track.

The visual echoes the feeling of wonder with a sense of uncertainty and anxiety created by the soundtrack. The unnatural movement of the bubbles, the flashy colours and the odd patterns produced an unreal atmosphere and enhanced the anxious feeling.

The hyper-realistic rendering also created an uncanny valley situation and made the whole video look like a psychedelic illusion, which is what life feels like sometimes.

Thank you to: Michael McKinnon, Terry Moore and Steven Lam for the technical support.

You can listen to the full track here.

The project was part of the course work of MDDN314: Audio-Visual Space for the Bachelor of Design Innovation (BDI) at Victoria University of Wellington (VUW).

Grounding Technique (2020)

Motion Graphics
Grounding Technique (2020)

I have a personal relationship and understanding of mental health and the pains and helplessness caused by it. Mental health is a topic that is still rather “tabooed”. People feel uncomfortable talking about it openly and avoid seeking help. Anxiety attacks are often silence and people tend to hide their problems. These will add up to their mental stress and may even cause people to react destructively.

The animation is a guide to doing a grounding technique for anxiety attacks, which primarily serve as a self-help aid. In the long term, I expect it to also serve as a reminder that help is out there and as a starting point for people to get help.

The animation is aimed at a general public, the planned broadcast of this animation is as an advertisement on television, theatres, and online videos. The animation can be reformatted to be placed on social media platforms such as Instagram and Facebook.

We were asked to attach our animation to an existing entity as an imaginary client for the project, I have chosen the Mental Health Foundation (MHF) of New Zealand, which is a charitable trust that promotes and advocate mental health and wellbeing in New Zealand. They often set up campaigns and services to aid people in New Zealand with their mental health needs.

The visual focus will be the use of animated icons and the colour scheme will be solid colours. The idea of using icons and solid colours is similar to the “All Right” campaign that Mental Health Foundation of New Zealand is currently collaborating with the Canterbury District Health Board. The reason to use a similar visual language is to keep the messages and style consistent. The animation is also related to the various messages that have been produced by the campaign.

The graphics will be created on the computer using illustration software. However, jitter effects will be added to the linework to create “rustic” frame-by-frame animation feel.

The script was based on the blog post “5-4-3-2-1 Coping Technique for Anxiety” published by the University of Rochester Medical Center in Rochester, New York. It was set up as a step by step instruction to exercise this technique.

Thanks to Billy Chang for the voice-over work.

The project was part of the course work of COMD 261: Motion Design I for the Bachelor of Design Innovation (BDI) at Victoria University of Wellington (VUW).

Breathe (2019)

Installation
Breathe (2019)

“We humans can never claim to do nothing, we breathe, we pulse, we regenerate.”

Suzanne Weyn, Mr. Magorium's Wonder Emporium

Breathe is a visualization of the respiratory rhythm of Wellington. Wellington is the world’s windiest city, and this piece uses the data of Wellington’s wind speed and direction to create a pulsing rhythm using light and shadows. The pulsing motion responses to the wind speed of Wellington and pulses faster when the speed increase. The lights also rotate and transit at a different rate when the direction on the wind changes.

I was inspired by shadow lamps where the shadows and the projected images are the key features. The irregular shape of Breathe gave it an organic feel.

The shape of the shadows is create using a combined mesh made up of pantyhose and paper packaging mesh. The exterior is made with cheesecloth with a piece of translucent plastic topping it off. The movement is created by using two micro-servos powered by an Arduino Uno, which is programmed to react to Wellington’s wind data.

Special Thanks to Anne Niemetz, Hunter Mulder, Michael McKinnon, Terry Moore, and Steven Lam for all the help and support.

The project was part of the course work of MDDN251: Physical Computing for the Bachelor of Design Innovation (BDI) at Victoria University of Wellington (VUW).

The Façade (2019)

Installation
The Façade (2019)

This was a kinetic art project done using the Arduino Uno microcontroller board. It was an abstract representation of the human psyche, and it was based on the “Honne and Tatemae” idea of the Japanese culture. The Façade has a simple and clean exterior that consists of 9 LEDs and a button that changes the colour of the LEDs. When no movement was detected, the front panel slides open and reveals its dark interior. It meant to represent how people put up a front in the presence of others as expected by society, but inevitably reveal their true inner self when no one is noticing.

Special Thanks to Anne Niemetz and Hunter Mulder for all the help and support.

The project was part of the course work of MDDN251: Physical Computing for the Bachelor of Design Innovation (BDI) at Victoria University of Wellington (VUW).

My Homes/Digital Impressionism (2019)

Coding
My Homes/Digital Impressionism (2019)
Previous Next
Click on image to view full size.

The idea to do show 2 places that I currently call home. The first image is shot at a decommissioned cross-country train track in Singapore The second is shot at the waterfront in Wellington, New Zealand. The last image is the flags of the two nations.

I was born and raised in Singapore. I left Singapore in 2018 to further my studies in New Zealand. The flight to Wellington was my first time taking a flight on my own with no friends and family members.

I have decided to create impressionism-like images as I wanted to do something that was traditionally hand-made but recreate it with a digital twist. Impressionist painting is often characterised by the visible form of the brush strokes. My work features different glyphs and symbols as "brush strokes" of the painting. This "brushstroke" are outlined to enhance the visibility of the individual glyphs.

The timing of this project is also quite coincidental as both nations signed a "NZ-Singapore Enhanced Partnership" agreement that year to strengthen the relation of both countries which I now call home.

The project was part of the course work of MDDN242: Creative Coding II for the Bachelor of Design Innovation (BDI) at Victoria University of Wellington (VUW).

One of the image was also shown on the digital billboard near the junction between Taranaki Street, Manners Street and Courtenay Place in Wellington, New Zealand as part of a student showcase for the University.

Loopable Animation (2019)

Loopable Animation (2019)

We were tasked to create a 35 seconds loopable animation using 5 "characters". 4 of these characters were given us with rigs and we were tasked to create a cohesive plot using these character. I created the box character using Autodesk Maya with elements sculpted using 3D-Coat.

The animation was built and rendered in Autodesk Maya and Arnold Renderer.

The sources of the rigged models are as follows:

  • Ultimate Walker and Ultimate Ball rigs by Ugur Ulvi Yetiskin
  • Flour Sack rig by Joe Daniels
  • Hotdog modelled by Caitlin Sue, rigged by Byron Mallett
  • Jeff by Byron Mallett

The project was part of the course work of MDDN241: 3D Modelling and Animation II for the Bachelor of Design Innovation (BDI) at Victoria University of Wellington (VUW).

Frozen Moment (2019)

3D Modelling
Frozen Moment (2019)
Click on image to view full size.

The initial idea started from seeing an animated image of a mobile phone dropping on the ground. The image makes me realise that a dropped technological device is pretty much a nightmare in the modern world in this day and age. We as people are so connected to our devices and the ability to have a “power of knowledge and information” is contained in a form that fits our hands. If the device breaks, the “power” is lost and destroyed.

I represented this using a translucent cube form that is dropped on the ground, breaking into pieces. The light represents the power of the cube. As the cube breaks, the power is diminished and lost. A blue colour palette is used as it is a peaceful and calm colour that provided a feeling of stillness The character was modelled with 3D-Coat and rendered in Autodesk Maya and Arnold Renderer.

The project was part of the course work of MDDN241: 3D Modelling and Animation II for the Bachelor of Design Innovation (BDI) at Victoria University of Wellington (VUW).

Weird Wellington (2018)

3D Modelling
Weird Wellington (2018)
Click on image to view full size.

The project brief required us to use only primitive forms, and inspiration from buildings and landmarks of Wellington to create a narrative scene to tell a story. My story was about a dystopian Wellington after a great earthquake that spilt the land with magma flowing at the break. The area separated into a wealthy half and poverty-stricken half. I also got inspiration from “Batman: The Animated Series” and worked in an Art Deco style to the visual language. The scene was modelled and rendered in Autodesk Maya and Arnold Renderer.

The project was part of the course work of DSDN132: 3D Modelling & Animation I for the Bachelor of Design Innovation (BDI) at Victoria University of Wellington (VUW).
